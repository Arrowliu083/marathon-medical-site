AED in Marathon Medical Support Standards

Having ≠ Effective

While AEDs are now widely accepted as essential equipment for marathon medical support — a significant advance from a decade ago — real-world events still suffer from three persistent failures: insufficient quantity, inability to locate devices quickly, and operators who lack the skills to use them. The standards go far beyond a simple requirement to "provide AEDs." They construct a complete deployment logic organized around one central question: how fast can a working AED reach a cardiac arrest victim on the course?

Quantity Requirements

The standards establish tiered minimum AED counts based on event type and participant numbers. For full marathons, the minimum ranges from 30 to 60 units (approximately one every 0.7–1.4 km). For half marathons, the range is 15 to 30 units. These are explicitly defined as minimums, not targets. The standards also provide for risk-adjusted flexibility: courses with switchbacks may justify fewer units, while irregular courses, poor visibility conditions, or extreme weather require additional deployment. The key principle is that every point on the course must fall within a viable AED response radius.

Mobile Carrying, Not Fixed Placement

Perhaps the most operationally significant requirement is that AEDs must be carried by on-course first responders or medical station staff — not simply placed at fixed locations. Cardiac arrest can occur anywhere: at an aid station, on an incline, at a turnaround, in the finishing straight, or between two medical stations. If responders must run to retrieve a fixed AED, precious minutes are lost. The standard's mobile-carrying requirement ensures that defibrillation capability moves with the risk.

The Six-Question AED Audit

The standards imply a six-part test for evaluating whether AED deployment is truly adequate: (1) Do the numbers meet the tiered density requirements? (2) Has the configuration been adjusted for course shape, weather, and responder response times? (3) Are AEDs carried by qualified personnel rather than stored at fixed points? (4) Have carriers been trained and drilled on the specific device models in use? (5) Can carriers communicate with the medical command center for coordinated dispatch? (6) Does every AED use generate a complete incident record enabling post-event review? Answering these six questions — not simply counting devices — is the purpose the standards serve.
