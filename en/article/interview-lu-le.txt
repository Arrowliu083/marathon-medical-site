b:Ta9d,## Thirteen Years on the Front Line

Lu Le, founder of First Response® and convener of the standards drafting expert group, has spent 13 years in marathon medical support. He also serves as an expert advisor to the Tokyo Marathon Medical Headquarters. His journey from a first-time rescuer to the architect of China's marathon medical standards is punctuated by three defining moments.

## Three Landmark Rescues

The first came on June 2, 2013, at the Shanghai Triathlon. A participant named Tingting suffered a sudden cardiac arrest during the race. The rapid on-scene response and timely defibrillation saved her life. For Lu Le, this was the moment he understood that event medical support is not a supporting service but a matter of life and death. The second landmark occurred on March 15, 2015, at the Wuxi Marathon — China's first successful on-course resuscitation of a marathon runner in cardiac arrest. Before Wuxi, cardiac arrest on a Chinese marathon course was effectively a death sentence. Wuxi proved that a properly organized medical support system could change that outcome. The third was March 12, 2017, at the Nagoya Women's Marathon in Japan, where three cardiac arrests among 20,000+ runners all resulted in successful resuscitation. Witnessing the Japanese system firsthand reinforced Lu Le's conviction: if Japan could do it, China could too — but only with standards, systems, and disciplined execution.

## The Art of Cross-Sector Collaboration

Drafting the standards required coordinating 38 experts from different provinces, different industries, and different professional cultures — health commission officials, emergency medicine specialists, race operations executives, and first-aid training pioneers. Building consensus among such a diverse group was itself a significant achievement. But it was precisely this diversity that gave the standards their strength: scientific rigor from the medical experts, operational practicality from the race operators, and enforceability from the regulatory officials.

## Useful, Not Beautiful

Lu Le's standards philosophy is uncompromisingly pragmatic. The standards were not written to look impressive on paper; they were written to work on the course. Every requirement traces back to real-world experience and documented cases. The numbers are not aspirational targets — they are calculated minimums, below which risk increases measurably. And the standards are not static: they are designed to evolve with new evidence, new technology, and new operational experience. The ultimate measure of success is simple: runners who start the race should go home safely.
