b:Tad1,## A Lifetime in Emergency Medicine

Professor Li Zonghao, former President of the China Association for Medical Rescue (CAMR) and head of the standards drafting expert group, has devoted 64 years to emergency medicine. He began his career at the Beijing Emergency Medical Center, serving as both a practicing physician and an administrator, and has participated in major disaster response operations and international academic exchanges throughout his career. In 2006, recognizing that China's hospital-centric medical rescue system was ill-equipped for pre-hospital emergency response, he proposed the establishment of a professional association to standardize and elevate the nation's medical rescue capabilities. The CAMR was approved by the Ministry of Civil Affairs in January 2009.

## Building the Standards Foundation

Under Professor Li's leadership, CAMR has developed and published over 120 standards spanning disaster rescue, pre-hospital emergency care, equipment specifications, aviation medicine, poisoning response, and trauma care — establishing itself as the core force in China's medical rescue standardization. When the Baiyin tragedy occurred in May 2021, CAMR and the Chinese Athletics Association jointly convened an expert group to develop specialized standards for marathon medical support. The result — T/CADERM 8001-2021 and T/CADERM 8002-2021 — was released at a December 2021 press conference in Beijing.

## A Vision for Cross-Sector Collaboration

In his Shaoxing symposium address, Professor Li expressed his hope that the standards would catalyze deeper cross-sector collaboration — between medical rescue professionals and sports administrators, between national experts and local implementers, between standard-setters and the running community. He noted with satisfaction the diversity of participants at the symposium: health commission officials, emergency physicians, race operators, elite runners, and first-aid trainers. This breadth of engagement, he suggested, is essential for standards to move from publication to practice.

## Serving the National Mission

Professor Li framed the standards effort within a larger context: serving the national fitness strategy and implementing the principle of "people first, life first." Marathon running, he observed, brings tremendous social value — encouraging physical activity, enhancing urban vitality, and promoting healthy lifestyles. But these benefits cannot be realized without a foundation of safety. The standards exist to ensure that every runner who stands at the start line can reasonably expect to return home safely.
