b:Tc36,## The Marathon-Specific Training Imperative

Liao Yukun, a World Athletics REMC Medical Officer who has directed medical support at over 500 events, identifies personnel training as the first critical area where standards application often falls short. His key insight: even experienced emergency physicians need marathon-specific training. A hospital emergency room and a marathon course are fundamentally different environments — the latter offers no diagnostic equipment, no team support, high ambient noise, and variable weather. The standards mandate pre-event training and drills not as a bureaucratic requirement but as an operational necessity. Training must cover course-specific communication equipment, AED operation on the specific models in use, heat stroke recognition and cooling protocols, team-based cardiac arrest response, and communication discipline with the command center.

## The Command Center: Brain of the System

The second area of concern is the medical command center. Many events include a command center in their planning documents, but in practice it may amount to little more than a tent with a walkie-talkie. The standards define specific requirements: a Chief Medical Officer with relevant event experience, a dedicated ambulance dispatcher, a communications specialist familiar with radio emergency protocols, independent communication systems, course monitoring capability, and genuine resource dispatch authority. When the command center functions as intended, it transforms scattered resources into an integrated system. When it does not, even abundant AEDs and ambulances underperform because no one is orchestrating their deployment.

## Incident Recording: The Foundation of Improvement

The third priority area is standardized incident documentation. Liao observes that many events produce incomplete medical records — missing critical time points such as when the incident was first detected, when CPR began, when defibrillation occurred, when the ambulance was loaded, and when the patient reached the hospital. Without this time chain, it is impossible to objectively assess rescue quality or identify process improvements for future events. The standards include a standardized emergency transport incident record form that captures these data points. Moreover, modern AEDs internally record event timelines, ECG data, and even ambient audio — information that is invaluable for post-event review but often overlooked by organizers unfamiliar with the technology.

## Rigor and Flexibility Combined

Liao's overarching message is that the standards embody a philosophy of "rigor with flexibility." The minimum requirements are firm — they represent the scientifically validated threshold below which safety is compromised. But the standards also explicitly invite upward adjustment based on risk assessment. This is not a contradiction; it is the essence of professional medical support.
