b:T9e1,## Every Second Counts

Zhang Yu, Medical Safety Director at Xinzhong Sports Group and a World Athletics REMC Medical Officer, brings an operator's pragmatism to the standards conversation. His core message is direct: on the marathon course, every second determines survival. The four-minute window for effective cardiac arrest response leaves no margin for delay in detection, identification, communication, CPR initiation, or defibrillation. A medical support system that cannot guarantee sub-minute response times across the entire course is, by definition, inadequate.

## Training Beyond Medical Staff

One of Zhang's signature approaches is universal training. In Xinzhong events, it is not only doctors and nurses who receive emergency training — volunteers, security personnel, and course marshals are all trained to recognize danger signs (abnormal gait, facial color changes, disorientation) and immediately report them via the communication system. This "everyone is a first responder" philosophy dramatically expands the safety net. The observation volunteer posted every 100 meters is not a passive presence but an active surveillance node in a distributed detection network.

## The Digital Command Platform

Technology is the third pillar of Zhang's approach. Xinzhong has invested in a digital command and dispatch platform that provides real-time visibility of every AED location, every ambulance status, and every first responder position. When an incident is reported, the system automatically identifies the nearest available resources and pushes navigation instructions to responders' devices. The platform also integrates real-time weather monitoring and is exploring UAV-based AI recognition to detect runners in distress before human observers can report them. The goal is full course digitization — every asset tracked, every decision data-driven.

## Continuous Iteration

The fourth pillar is institutionalized learning. After every event, Xinzhong conducts a detailed medical debrief, analyzing every incident, every near-miss, and every process deviation. These findings feed back into the standards revision cycle, ensuring that the standards evolve with operational experience. Zhang emphasizes that sustainable medical support requires stable collaboration mechanisms among government, race companies, medical institutions, and emergency response organizations.
