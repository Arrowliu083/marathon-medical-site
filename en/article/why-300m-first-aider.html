Why the Standard Requires a First Aider Every 300 Meters

Liao Yukun
Standards Drafting Expert / World Athletics REMC Medical Officer
Published
2026-05-24

T/CADERM 8001-2021 Section 6.2.2 stipulates: "On course segments with participants, on-course first aiders shall be spaced no more than 300m apart."

Core Purpose: Responding to Cardiac Arrest

Cardiac arrest can occur anywhere on the course, at any time, to any runner — including those who are otherwise healthy. Every minute of delay reduces survival probability by 7–10%. Based on the American Heart Association Chain of Survival and years of research, defibrillation within 4 minutes yields survival rates above 80%. Data from standards drafting organizations shows cardiac arrest survival rates ≥92% when an effective medical support system is in place.

For event organizers, cardiac arrest response is the central mission of the medical support system. The 300m spacing requirement serves this time-critical need first and foremost.

Achieving the "golden four minutes" on the course is the fundamental rationale behind the first aider density requirement.

The operational breakdown: first aiders without AEDs should reach the victim within 2 minutes; those carrying AEDs within 3 minutes; and the first defibrillation should be delivered within 4 minutes (a trained first aider should need no more than 1 minute to operate the AED upon arrival).

With 300m spacing, any collapsed runner is typically no more than 150m from the nearest first aider. First aiders without AEDs focus on rapid discovery, activating medical command or EMS, and initiating immediate CPR. AED-carrying first aiders, cycling AED teams, medical stations, or ambulances then follow through with defibrillation and advanced care. In essence: the 300m personnel density compresses the "discovery to CPR" window, while AED deployment compresses the "CPR to defibrillation" window.

Preventing: Heat-Related Illness Concurrency

Two points demand organizers' attention regarding heat illness:

1. Heat illness can occur even at relatively low temperatures during prolonged endurance exercise, with risk escalating as temperature rises. 2. Heat illness events tend to cluster in time and location — particularly when temperatures exceed 22°C or WBGT > 18, creating a risk of multiple concurrent incidents.

Under concurrency conditions, 300m spacing preserves essential redundancy. When one first aider is attending to a casualty, adjacent first aiders continue monitoring the course and can respond to subsequent incidents, preventing the medical chain from being "pierced" by simultaneous events.

Risks of 1km AED Responder Deployment

Many events currently deploy bicycle or e-scooter AED responders at 1km intervals. While seemingly efficient, this leaves any casualty at least 500m from the nearest responder — too far for timely discovery and immediate response. When communication delays and location-finding time are factored in, achieving the golden four minutes becomes virtually impossible. Furthermore, when one responder is occupied, a gap exceeding 1km opens in the response coverage, creating unacceptable delays for concurrent incidents.

Can Observer Volunteers Replace First Aiders?

Under the Chinese Athletics Association's five-layer protection system, medical observer volunteers provide density advantages (typically deployed at 100m intervals). However, if these volunteers only observe and report — without valid first aid certification, without passing on-course competency assessments, unable to perform CPR and AED operation, and not integrated into the medical communications and command system — they cannot substitute for the 300m first aider requirement.

Conversely, if within a 100m observer system, at least one person every 300m fully meets the standard's requirements — age 18+, valid certification, competency verification, CPR/AED/emergency communication/privacy protection skills, and integration into the position numbering, communications, command, and recording system — then these personnel may count as first aiders. Observer volunteers strengthen the discovery network but cannot replace first aid capability with observation alone.

What to Do When First Aiders Are in Short Supply

For regions with limited first aider resources:

  • Begin medical support organization early, allowing sufficient time for recruitment and training (e.g., Dalian Marathon, Chongqing Marathon prepare months in advance).
  • For recurring events, establish long-term mechanisms for first aider training and reserve through partnerships with local hospitals, emergency centers, Red Cross, AHA training sites, rescue teams, running clubs, and universities.
  • Use both public recruitment and targeted recruitment channels.
  • Commission capable partners to execute on-course first aid modules.
  • Leverage first-aid runners who meet the standard's competency requirements and are equipped with necessary resources (communications, etc.).
Above all, a stable medical support team or core personnel is essential — whether preparing for this year's event or building medical support resources for the long term.
