The Shaoxing Symposium

In early 2026, a landmark industry symposium on marathon medical support standards convened in Shaoxing, Zhejiang Province. Over 40 participants — including standards drafting experts, race operators, elite runners, Red Cross representatives, and Blue Sky Rescue members — gathered to discuss standard promotion, operational feedback, and the roadmap for iterative improvement.

Shaoxing's Exemplary Record

The choice of Shaoxing as host city was deliberate. Since its inaugural event in 2017, the Shaoxing Marathon has completed eight editions with a perfect safety record: zero fatalities. Pan Liangliang, Deputy Director of Shaoxing's Health Commission, presented the "Shaoxing Model," detailing the city's organizational framework, multi-layered defense architecture, pre-race preparation protocols, and emergency response procedures. The model demonstrates that sustained excellence in medical support is achievable when standards are embedded in local practice.

Expert Voices

The symposium featured a series of substantive expert contributions. Dr. Cai Wenwei of Zhejiang Provincial Emergency Command Center analyzed sudden cardiac death risk patterns, noting China's marathon sudden death rate of 0.8 per 100,000 — comparable to the United States — with 90% being cardiac in origin and the 24-hour post-race window representing a high-risk period for delayed events. Professor Lu Yiming of Shanghai Ruijin Hospital proposed three optimization directions: mandatory pre-race health screening, AI-enhanced command systems, and bringing ECPR (extracorporeal cardiopulmonary resuscitation) capability to the course. Gu Bangchao of Jiangsu Provincial Health Commission advocated for the "first witness" concept and emphasized the critical role of the Chief Medical Officer position defined in the standards.

The Data Behind the System

First Response®, a leading marathon medical support provider, reported a cumulative cardiac arrest survival rate of 92% across 453 events in 40 Chinese cities and 115 events in 12 Japanese cities over ten years. This statistic — nearly triple the typical out-of-hospital cardiac arrest survival rate — validates the core premise of the standards effort: that systematized, standardized medical support transforms survival outcomes. The symposium concluded with a clear consensus: China's marathon medical support is transitioning from experience-driven improvisation to system-driven reliability, and the two standards are the foundation of that transition.
